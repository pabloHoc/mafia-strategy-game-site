# District Axes

**Status:** WIP

## Summary

District Axes are a small set of district-level characteristics that define the strategic identity of a district.

They are not resources, stats to optimize, or faction-specific bonuses. Instead, they represent the social, political, economic, and criminal conditions of a district.

Axes change slowly over time through player actions, organizational presence, assets, and major events. They are intentionally difficult to manipulate directly, making them long-term strategic commitments rather than tactical switches.

The primary purpose of axes is to create meaningful differences between districts, force localized planning, and enable multiple strategic paths toward similar goals.

---

# Design Goals

## Make Location Matter

The same mission, asset, or organization should behave differently depending on where it is used.

Players should think:

> "Where should I do this?"

not simply:

> "Should I do this?"

---

## Create District Identity

Districts should develop recognizable personalities and strategic roles.

Examples:

- Financial hub
- Criminal haven
- Oppressive stronghold
- Rebellious slums

The goal is for districts to feel different from each other rather than becoming interchangeable territory containers.

---

## Encourage Long-Term Planning

Axes should change slowly and be difficult to reverse.

Players should be shaping districts over many turns to support future plans.

---

## Support Multiple Strategies

Different district states should enable different approaches to success.

There should rarely be a universally optimal district configuration.

---

## Enable Indirect Conflict

Players should be able to influence enemy-controlled districts by manipulating their axes.

Strategic disruption should be possible without directly conquering territory.

---

# What District Axes Should Be

## Strategic Terrain

Axes represent the environment in which criminal activity takes place.

They function similarly to terrain in strategy games.

Players adapt to them, shape them, and exploit them.

---

## Long-Term Commitments

District states should have inertia.

Changing an established district identity should require significant effort and time.

Districts should develop history.

---

## Sources of Opportunities

Axes should unlock:

- Organizations
- Missions
- Assets
- Opportunities
- Strategic approaches

Their primary role is determining what is possible, not merely what is efficient.

---

## Meaningful Tradeoffs

Every axis direction must provide advantages and disadvantages.

Neither side of an axis should be strictly superior.

---

# What District Axes Should NOT Be

## Not Numerical Optimization Puzzles

Avoid:

- +5% bonuses
- +10% modifiers
- Constant stat tuning

Axes should not become spreadsheets.

---

## Not Maintenance Mechanics

Players should not need to constantly manage district values every turn.

Avoid gameplay such as:

- Repeatedly restoring lost points
- Constant axis upkeep
- Micromanaging decay

---

## Not Directly Adjustable Sliders

Players should rarely choose:

> Increase Fear +1

Instead, axes should mostly change as consequences of meaningful actions.

---

## Not Universal Progress Bars

Axes should not function as:

- Development score
- District level
- Overall quality indicator

There should not be a "best" district state.

---

# Structure

## Small Number of Axes

Target:

- 3-4 total axes

Maximum:

- 4

Every additional axis increases complexity across:

- Missions
- Organizations
- Assets
- AI behavior
- District management
- Balance

Axes must justify their existence.

---

## Discrete States

Prefer a small number of meaningful stages.

Example:

- State 1
- State 2
- State 3
- State 4

Rather than large numerical scales.

The focus should be on crossing meaningful thresholds.

---

# Axis Thresholds

Axes should create qualitative changes.

Avoid:

Fear 1 = +5%
Fear 2 = +10%
Fear 3 = +15%

Prefer:

Fear 1:
- Calm

Fear 2:
- Intimidated

Fear 3:
- Terrorized

Fear 4:
- Oppressed

Each threshold should unlock new opportunities, risks, or constraints.

Players should care about reaching specific states.

---

# How Axes Change

## Missions

Actions leave lasting consequences.

Examples:

- Extortion increases Fear
- Smuggling increases Corruption
- Gang wars increase Chaos
- Community investment increases Order

Players shape districts through activity.

---

## Assets

Assets exert ongoing influence.

Examples:

- Casino
- Safe House
- Church
- Smuggling Hub

Assets help define district identity over time.

---

## Organizations

Organizations naturally pull districts toward particular states.

This makes organizational control strategically meaningful beyond simple bonuses.

---

## Special Operations

Dedicated district-shaping actions may exist.

Examples:

- Destabilize District
- Anti-Corruption Campaign
- Crackdown
- Public Works Program

These should be:

- Expensive
- Slow
- Visible

They are exceptions, not the primary way axes change.

---

# Axis Momentum

Districts resist dramatic change.

The more entrenched a district state becomes, the harder it is to reverse.

Example:

Ordered IV
→ Ordered III (easy)

Ordered III
→ Ordered II (moderate)

Ordered II
→ Ordered I (hard)

Ordered I
→ Chaotic I (very hard)

This creates permanence and district history.

---

# Strategic Interactions

## Unlocking Content

Certain content may require specific district states.

Examples:

- Smuggling Networks
- Political Machines
- Revolutionary Cells
- Protection Rackets

Players may need to shape a district before executing their intended strategy.

---

## District Specialization

Not every district should support every strategy equally.

Axes encourage districts to specialize.

Players create networks of districts serving different purposes.

---

## Multiple Paths to Success

Different district states should support different approaches toward similar goals.

Example:

Wealth Generation:

Ordered District:
- Legitimate businesses
- Political influence
- Financial operations

Chaotic District:
- Smuggling
- Protection rackets
- Black markets

Both succeed through different methods.

---

## Emergent Opportunities

Organizations, missions, events, and opportunities may appear only under certain district conditions.

Examples:

- Criminal Syndicates
- Civic Associations
- Street Gangs
- Merchant Guilds

Axes help generate unique district stories and opportunities.

---

## Indirect Warfare

Players can attack enemy districts by targeting their axes.

Examples:

- Spread panic
- Sow unrest
- Encourage corruption
- Destabilize institutions

Damaging a district's identity may sometimes be easier than conquering it directly.

This creates strategic alternatives and comeback opportunities.

---

# Core Design Rule

District Axes should primarily determine:

> What is possible in a district.

They should only secondarily determine:

> How effective something is.

Unlocking opportunities, constraints, and strategic paths is generally more interesting than providing numerical bonuses.

## Open Questions

- What are the final 3-4 axes?
- Are axes opposites on a single track or independent states?
- What are the meaningful threshold states for each axis?
- What opportunities, restrictions, and tradeoffs does each threshold create?
- How can players intentionally influence friendly and enemy district axes?
- How much inertia should districts have, and how difficult should it be to reverse an established identity?
- Should districts naturally drift over time or only change through player/world actions?
- What rare organizations, assets, missions, or opportunities emerge from specific district states or combinations?
- How are district identities communicated to the player without relying on numerical values?
- How should AI factions shape and exploit district identities?
